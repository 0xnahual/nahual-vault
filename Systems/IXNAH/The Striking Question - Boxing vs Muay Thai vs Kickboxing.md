# The Striking Question — Boxing vs Muay Thai vs Kickboxing
*Which striking art belongs in IXNAH? Should kicks stay? What would I actually do?*

---

## THE QUESTION

You already have:
- **Judo** — the combat backbone. Throws, trips, pins, breakfalls, grip fighting, randori under pressure. This stays. Non-negotiable.
- **TKD kicks** — documented in your files. Front push kick, roundhouse, side kick, spinning back kick, axe kick.
- **Empty boxing files** — Jab, Recto, Hook pages exist but contain nothing.
- **The IXNAH system flow** — Strike → Entry → Grip → Kuzushi → Throw → End

The question is: what fills the "Strike → Entry" gap? Boxing (hands only)? Muay Thai (hands + kicks + elbows + knees + clinch)? Kickboxing (hands + kicks)? And should kicks stay in IXNAH at all?

---

## THE THREE OPTIONS — HONEST COMPARISON

### Option 1: Boxing (Hands Only)

**What you get:**
- Jab, cross, hook, uppercut — the 4 fundamental hand strikes
- Head movement — slip, roll, pull back, weave
- Footwork — lateral movement, angles, cutting off the ring
- Distance management — the most important combat skill
- Timing and rhythm — when to throw, when to wait
- Defense — parry, catch, shoulder roll, guard

**What you DON'T get:**
- No kicks
- No elbows
- No knees
- No clinch striking (boxing clinch is a reset, not a weapon)

**Time to competence:** 6-12 months to look decent on a bag. 12-18 months to hold your own in light sparring.

**How it fits IXNAH:**
Boxing fills the "Strike" layer perfectly. Jab-cross enters range → clinch → judo grip → throw. Clean pipeline. No wasted techniques.

**The case FOR boxing:**
1. **Your hands are the gap.** Your legs are trained through stances, TKD kicks, and judo footwork. Your hands are empty. Boxing fixes the weakest link.
2. **Simplicity.** 4 punches, 3 defensive movements, footwork. That's it. You can learn the fundamentals in weeks, not years.
3. **It works under pressure.** Boxing is the most pressure-tested striking art in history. Every technique has been validated in thousands of professional fights.
4. **It bridges to judo naturally.** Miss the cross → step in → underhook → judo grip. This is how judo fighters close distance in MMA. Boxing is the on-ramp.
5. **Heavy bag at home.** You can train boxing alone with a bag, timer, and your phone recording. No training partner needed. Fits your solo training model.
6. **Speed of learning.** You can have functional boxing in 6 months. Muay Thai takes longer because there are more weapons.

**The case AGAINST boxing:**
1. **No kicks = incomplete striker.** If someone throws a low kick at you and you've never trained kicks, you won't know how to check it, absorb it, or return one.
2. **No clinch offense.** Boxing clinch = referee separates you. Real clinch = knees, elbows, dirty boxing. Boxing doesn't teach this.
3. **Limited range.** Boxing operates at punching distance only. Kicks operate at a longer range. You lose that entire zone.

---

### Option 2: Muay Thai (The Full Weapon System)

**What you get:**
- Punches — jab, cross, hook (less refined than boxing, but functional)
- Kicks — low kick (the destroyer), body kick, head kick, teep (push kick)
- Elbows — horizontal, diagonal, spinning, uppercut elbow
- Knees — straight knee, clinch knee, flying knee
- Clinch — the Muay Thai clinch is a weapon, not a reset. Double collar tie → knee strikes. Trips and sweeps from clinch.

**What you DON'T get:**
- Boxing-level hand skills. Muay Thai punching is functional but lacks the precision, angles, and defensive head movement of pure boxing.
- Boxing-level footwork. Muay Thai stance is more planted (to check kicks and throw kicks). Less lateral movement.

**Time to competence:** 12-18 months to look decent. 18-24 months to spar.

**How it fits IXNAH:**
Muay Thai fills the "Strike → Entry" layer AND adds clinch striking that bridges directly into judo. The Muay Thai plum (double collar tie) → knee is one step away from judo's collar grip → throw.

**The case FOR Muay Thai:**
1. **Most complete striking art.** 8 weapons (2 fists, 2 elbows, 2 knees, 2 shins) vs boxing's 2 (fists). You get everything.
2. **The clinch bridges to judo.** Muay Thai clinch fighting → judo grip fighting is a natural transition. You're already in body-to-body range. This is the strongest argument for Muay Thai.
3. **Low kicks.** The low kick is arguably the most effective strike in a real fight. It damages the leg, slows the opponent, doesn't risk much. Boxing has no answer for this.
4. **Kick defense.** You learn to CHECK kicks (shin block), which is critical if anyone ever throws a kick at you.
5. **Your TKD kicks become useful.** You already have documented kicks. Muay Thai makes them battle-tested by adding power generation and application under pressure.
6. **The teep.** Front push kick = distance management with the leg. Keeps people away. You can land it without ever entering punching range. Perfect for someone who doesn't want to take punches.

**The case AGAINST Muay Thai:**
1. **Too many weapons, slower mastery.** Boxing gives you functional hands in 6 months. Muay Thai gives you mediocre everything in 12 months. Jack of all trades problem.
2. **Your HANDS are the gap, not your kicks.** You can already kick (TKD). You CAN'T punch. Why train a system that's only 30% hands when your hands need 100% of the work?
3. **Harder to train solo.** Muay Thai clinch requires a partner. Knee strikes on a bag are awkward without Thai pads. Shadow kickboxing is less productive than shadow boxing.
4. **Injury risk from sparring.** Muay Thai sparring includes leg kicks. Getting your legs chewed up affects your judo, your stances, your running, everything. Boxing sparring is (relatively) safer.
5. **Stance conflict with judo.** Muay Thai stance is wide and planted. Judo stance is upright and mobile. Training both simultaneously can create conflicting motor patterns in the first 1-2 years.

---

### Option 3: Kickboxing (Hands + Kicks, No Clinch)

**What you get:**
- Boxing-quality hands (many kickboxing gyms are basically boxing + kicks)
- Kicks — roundhouse, front kick, side kick, spinning kicks
- Footwork that works at both punching and kicking range

**What you DON'T get:**
- No elbows
- No knees
- No clinch work (in most rulesets, clinch is broken up)

**Time to competence:** 8-14 months. Slightly faster than Muay Thai because fewer weapons.

**How it fits IXNAH:**
Kickboxing fills the "Strike" layer with both hands and feet but leaves the "Entry → Grip" transition empty. You still need judo to bridge the gap.

**The case FOR kickboxing:**
1. **Best of both worlds.** Better hands than Muay Thai, kicks included.
2. **Your TKD kicks port directly.** The kicks you already know ARE kickboxing kicks. You just need to add hands.
3. **Dutch kickboxing** specifically is famous for hand-heavy fighting with low kicks. This style is essentially boxing + low kicks. Might be perfect.

**The case AGAINST kickboxing:**
1. **No clinch.** The clinch is where your judo takes over. Kickboxing doesn't teach you how to GET to the clinch. You need something that enters close range.
2. **Gym availability.** Pure kickboxing gyms are rarer than boxing or Muay Thai gyms. You're more likely to find "Muay Thai" or "boxing" near you.
3. **Identity crisis.** Kickboxing sits between boxing and Muay Thai without being better at either. It's the compromise.

## SHOULD KICKS STAY IN IXNAH?

**Yes. Absolutely. Here's why:**

### 1. A "capable human" must be able to use all limbs as weapons.

Your definition: capable of moving, of violence, of defending, of strength, of longevity. A capable human who can only punch is not fully capable. Someone throws a low kick at you — what do you do? Someone is on the ground — you can't soccer kick? You're at long range — you can only close distance with your hands?

Kicks stay because capability demands it.

### 2. You already have kick infrastructure.

You documented TKD kicks. Your stances (Ma Bu, Gong Bu) build the leg strength and hip flexibility required for kicks. Your training generates kick power without you even trying. Throwing kicks away would be throwing away an asset you already built.

### 3. Kicks connect to your judo.

Judo has ashi-waza — foot techniques. Osoto gari, kouchi gari, ouchi gari, deashi barai. These are not kicks, but the body mechanics overlap. The hip rotation in a roundhouse kick and the hip rotation in harai goshi share the same engine. Training kicks reinforces judo mechanics.

### 4. The teep/front push kick is the most IXNAH-appropriate strike.

It creates distance. It doesn't require closing range. It's defensive. It uses the legs (your strong point). It's the martial art equivalent of "stay back." For someone who doesn't want to fight but must be capable of it — the teep is the first tool.

### 5. Low kicks are the most practical self-defense strike that exists.

A low kick to the thigh from a trained person drops an untrained person instantly. It's safe (you can't break your hand like punching someone's skull), effective (dead leg = can't chase you), and creates distance. IXNAH's self-defense philosophy should center on this.

**But kicks need to be IXNAH kicks, not TKD exhibition kicks.**

Remove from IXNAH:
- Spinning hook kick (Dwi Huryeo Chagi) — show-off kick, no practical value
- Axe kick (Naeryo Chagi) — telegraphed, easy to dodge, risky
- Any jumping kick — acrobatics, not combat

Keep in IXNAH:
- **Teep / front push kick** — distance management, the "jab of the legs"
- **Low roundhouse kick** — leg destruction, the most practical strike
- **Body roundhouse kick** — power strike, fight-ending capability
- **Side kick** — long range power, structural integrity test
- **Sweep** — not a kick per se, but foot-to-foot disruption (deashi barai territory)

**The rule: if you wouldn't throw it in a real fight at 40 years old, it doesn't belong in IXNAH.**

## WHAT I WOULD DO IN YOUR POSITION

Here is exactly what I would do, given:
- You're 89kg, training daily, learning judo
- You have stances, kick knowledge, zero functional hand skills
- You're an engineer who trains solo most of the time
- You have a heavy bag (or can get one)
- You hate wasted motion and inefficiency
- IXNAH needs to be trainable for life, not just competitive at 30

### The Decision: Boxing First, Muay Thai Elements Later

**Phase 1 — Year 1 (2026): Boxing + Judo**

Train boxing. Not Muay Thai. Not kickboxing. Boxing.

Why:
- Your hands are at ZERO. Your kicks are at 60%. Fix the zero.
- Boxing is faster to learn. 4 punches, head movement, footwork. You can be functional in 6 months.
- A heavy bag in your house is the perfect training tool. 3 rounds, 3 minutes each, 2-3x per week. 15 minutes of work. Done.
- Boxing's defense (head movement, guard, parry) is the best striking defense that exists. You'll need this before anything else.

What this looks like weekly:
```
Monday: Judo
Tuesday: Heavy bag — 3×3min boxing rounds + shadow boxing
Wednesday: Training (stances, calisthenics, IXNAH)
Thursday: Judo
Friday: Heavy bag — 3×3min + kick work on bag (use your existing kicks)
Saturday: Training (stances, calisthenics, IXNAH)
Sunday: Rest or light mobility
```

Notice: Friday includes kick work. You're not abandoning kicks. You're just giving hands PRIORITY because they need it.

**Phase 2 — Year 2 (2027): Add Muay Thai Weapons to Boxing Base**

Once your hands are functional (you can throw jab-cross-hook-uppercut with power and flow, and you can slip and roll):

Add to your bag work:
- **Low kick** — after a jab-cross, throw a low kick. This is the Dutch kickboxing bread and butter.
- **Teep** — use it as a range finder before punching combinations
- **Clinch knee** — after entering range with punches, clinch the bag, throw knees
- **Elbows** — close range, after a missed hook, elbow

You're NOT enrolling in a Muay Thai gym. You're adding Muay Thai WEAPONS to your boxing base through bag work. The distinction matters. Your fundamentals are boxing. Your supplementary weapons are Muay Thai.

**Phase 3 — Year 3+ (2028+): Integration**

By now your IXNAH combat flow works:

```
Teep (distance management)
  → Jab-Cross (close distance)
    → Low kick or body kick (if they retreat)
    → OR clinch entry (if they stand)
      → Underhook / collar tie
        → Knee strikes (Muay Thai)
          → Judo grip (kuzushi)
            → Throw
              → Pin or standup
```

That's the complete pipeline. Every phase filled by a proven art:
- Long range: kicks (Muay Thai/TKD)
- Mid range: punches (boxing)
- Close range: clinch (Muay Thai + judo)
- Grappling: throws and pins (judo)

### Why NOT Muay Thai First?

Because Muay Thai gyms will have you kicking from day 1 and punching with mediocre technique. Your kicks are already ahead. Your hands are behind. If you walk into a Muay Thai gym, you'll spend 60% of class doing things you can already do (kicks, knees, stance) and 40% on what you actually need (hands, head movement).

Boxing gym = 100% of the time on your weakness.

After 12 months of boxing, you'll have better hands than most Muay Thai fighters. THEN you layer on the kicks and knees you already understand.

### Why NOT Kickboxing?

Because it doesn't exist as a distinct path in most cities. You'll find "Muay Thai" gyms and "boxing" gyms. "Kickboxing" is usually one of those two with a different label. And the Dutch style you'd want is rare outside of the Netherlands.

Also: kickboxing doesn't teach clinch. The clinch is where your judo comes alive. You need a system that ENTERS the clinch, not one that avoids it.

### The Solo Training Model

This is the real reason boxing wins for you. You're an engineer. You train alone. A heavy bag is your best friend.

**What you can train alone on a heavy bag:**
- Boxing: everything. All 4 punches, all combinations, all defense (shadow boxing), bag rounds.
- Kicks: everything. Low kick, body kick, teep, side kick — all trainable on a bag.
- Muay Thai clinch: NOTHING. You need a partner. A bag doesn't clinch back.
- Muay Thai pad work: NOTHING. You need a partner holding pads.

Boxing is the solo training art. Judo is the partner training art. Together they cover everything — boxing alone, judo at the dojo.

Muay Thai requires partners for its best training methods (pad work, clinch sparring). If you're not enrolling in a Muay Thai gym 3x/week, the clinch and pad work won't develop. The kicks and knees you CAN train alone on the bag.

**Verdict: Train boxing as the striking art. Add Muay Thai weapons (low kick, teep, knee, elbow) through solo bag work. Keep judo as the grappling/clinch art. Kicks stay in IXNAH.**

## THE IXNAH STRIKING CURRICULUM — WHAT BELONGS

Based on all of this, here's what IXNAH's striking should look like:

### IXNAH Hands (from Boxing)
1. **Jab** — lead hand straight, distance control
2. **Cross** — rear hand straight, power shot
3. **Hook** — lead hand circular, close range power
4. **Uppercut** — rising strike, inside range

### IXNAH Kicks (from Muay Thai/TKD, filtered for practicality)
1. **Teep** — front push kick, distance management
2. **Low roundhouse** — leg attack, the most useful kick
3. **Body roundhouse** — power kick, targets ribs/liver
4. **Side kick** — long range push, structural power

### IXNAH Dirty Range (from Muay Thai)
1. **Clinch knee** — double collar tie → straight knee
2. **Horizontal elbow** — after entering clinch range
3. **Diagonal elbow** — following a missed hook

### IXNAH Defense (from Boxing + Muay Thai)
1. **Guard** — high hands, chin tucked
2. **Slip** — move head off centerline to avoid straight punches
3. **Roll** — duck under hooks
4. **Parry** — redirect punches with hands
5. **Check** — shin block for incoming kicks
6. **Teep as defense** — front kick to create space

### What's NOT in IXNAH
- Spinning kicks (telegraphed, risky, ego-driven)
- Axe kicks (impractical outside competition)
- Jump kicks (acrobatics)
- Superman punch (show-off move)
- Any technique that doesn't work at age 50

---

## THE FINAL WORD

IXNAH is for the capable human. A capable human can punch and kick. The question was never "kicks or no kicks." The question was "what kicks, and from what source?"

Answer: boxing hands + practical kicks from Muay Thai, filtered through the IXNAH principle of lifelong capability. Learn boxing first because your hands are at zero. Keep your kicks, refine them through bag work. Let judo handle everything from the clinch onward.

Train boxing this year. Add the rest as your hands catch up to your legs. By year 3, the system is complete.

Judo is the backbone. Boxing is the fists. Muay Thai weapons are the edge. IXNAH is the container that holds all three.
