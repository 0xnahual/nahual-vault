# The Music Strategy — Ricardo Bustos
### Two projects, no grind, no brand anxiety. Just make the thing.

---

## THE CORE INSIGHT FIRST

You have been trying to solve a branding problem when you actually have a clarity problem.

The branding question — "how do I promote this, how do I build an audience, what's my content strategy" — is the wrong question. It's the question you ask when you don't know what the thing IS yet. Once you know what the thing is, the branding is obvious.

So this document doesn't start with strategy. It starts with clarity. What are these two projects, actually, separately, honestly.

---

## PROJECT 1: YOUR PERSONAL MUSIC

### What it actually is

Folk. Vulnerable. Spanish. Your voice, a guitar, real feeling. Songs written from specific emotional moments in your life. "De chiquito" — the most honest thing in your vault. Songs that exist because something needed to be said, not because a universe needed a soundtrack.

This is not Ixtlan. This is Ricardo Bustos talking.

### The shame question

You're ashamed of "de chiquito" because it feels soft, because you don't say bad words anymore, because you're not that person in depression who wrote it.

Here's the reframe: **that song is not about weakness. Read the lyrics again.**

> *"Y aunque la vida me ha tratado bien culero / Yo si soy yo, Yo sigo aqui, Yo no me fui"*

That's not a depressed person. That's someone who got hit and didn't leave. That's resilience, not fragility. The person who wrote that was fighting. You just can't see it from the inside because you lived it.

Also — you don't have to perform it live if it doesn't feel true anymore. A song can exist in the world without being your live set. Release it because it's real, not because it defines who you are now.

### The new songs

The person you are now — post-transformation, post-job, five years of training, 38kg down, building things — he hasn't written his songs yet. Or he has, and they're in the maquetas pile waiting to be finished.

The current you has things to say that the depression-era you couldn't. Those are the songs to write. Not better, not more commercial — just more true to right now.

### What to do with the personal music

**Release it like it's a journal, not a launch.**

No campaign. No strategy deck. No content calendar. A song is done — it goes on Spotify. You share it once, wherever feels natural. That's it. No follow-up posts. No "link in bio." No promotion plan. Just: the song exists now.

This is how Ed Maverick built his entire career. Recorded songs in his room, put them on SoundCloud, they spread because they were real. Not because he had a strategy. He still records like that.

The model: **make → finish → release → forget → make the next one.**

The "finish" part is the only real problem. See the section on maquetas below.

## PROJECT 2: IXTLAN

### What it actually is

Electronic rock. Cinematic. Muse energy. The soundtrack to a universe — a 22nd century world where magic is suppressed, where nine pillars of consciousness determine power, where William becomes the Nahual, where the Void whispers "¿cuántas veces más vas a intentar salvarte?"

This is not Ricardo Bustos talking. This is a world talking.

Ixtlan is not a movement, not a brand, not a content strategy. It's a universe. Specifically: **a game with a soundtrack.** That's the simplest, most honest description of what it is.

### The relationship between Ixtlan music and the game

The music IS the game's world before the game exists. Every Ixtlan track is a piece of lore. "Fearless" is the Firebearer's theme. "Limitless" is William's awakening. "Spirits Never Die" is what the Temple Nahual sounds like.

When the game releases, people who've heard the music will feel like they're entering a world they already know. This is exactly how Undertale, Hollow Knight, and Hades built pre-launch communities — the music existed first, the world felt real before you could play it.

This means: **Ixtlan music releases are world-building, not promotion.** You're not making content. You're building a universe one song at a time.

### The production problem

You stopped Ixtlan because you were doing everything — songwriting, production, mixing, engineering — alone, and ran out of money. The job fixes the money part. But doing everything alone is still the bottleneck.

The solution is in the maquetas section below.

### What to do with Ixtlan

**Treat it as the game's expanding universe.** Every song that releases is accompanied by one piece of lore — a character description, a world fragment, a piece of the story. Not a content strategy. Just context. "This is the Firebearer's theme. He's a warrior whose power comes from will. He burns brightest when he's about to lose."

That's it. No campaign. No aesthetic grid. The lore IS the content.

Pace: one Ixtlan track every 2-3 months. Slow. Intentional. Each one a piece of the world.

## THE MAQUETAS PROBLEM — HOW TO ACTUALLY FINISH SONGS

You have a shit ton of maquetas. Chords + melody but no finished production. Unfinished FL Studio files. The production gap is the only thing standing between you and music that exists.

### For personal songs: stop producing, start recording

Your personal folk songs don't need FL Studio. They need your voice and a guitar (or piano, or minimal arrangement). The production should be simple by design — that's the genre.

**The process:**
1. Pick the maqueta that feels most alive right now — not the oldest, not the most finished, the one with the most feeling
2. Record the vocal and guitar/chord progression cleanly. One take, maybe two. Don't overdo it.
3. Send it to a mixing engineer on Fiverr or SoundBetter. $100-200. Tell them: "intimate folk, minimal, the voice is everything." Let them finish it.
4. Master it (same engineer or LANDR for $10). Done.
5. Upload to Spotify via DistroKid ($22/year, unlimited releases).

The personal songs don't need you to become a great producer. They need your voice recorded well and someone else to make it sound good. That's it. You've been trying to do a job that isn't yours for this project.

### For Ixtlan: find one collaborator

Ixtlan is electronic rock. That's a production-heavy genre. You doing it alone is why it stalled. The answer isn't to learn more FL Studio — it's to find one person who loves the world and can produce it with you.

This is not hiring someone. This is finding a collaborator — someone who hears the Ixtlan concept and gets excited. A producer who wants to be part of building a universe, not just complete a job.

Where to find them: Reddit (r/WeAreTheMusicMakers, r/gamedev), Discord servers for indie game music, SoundBetter collaborations section, even Twitter/X reaching out to producers who make music in the Muse/Radiohead/cinematic rock space.

What to offer: "I'm building a game universe. I have songs written. I need a producer who wants to co-create the soundtrack. Revenue share when it makes money, credit on everything, you're building something real."

One good collaborator changes everything for Ixtlan. You bring the world, the lyrics, the vocal, the vision. They bring the production. That's a complete team.

**Until you find that person:** release the Ixtlan tracks you already have on Spotify at their current state if they're listenable, with a note that the full production is in development. Or leave Ixtlan paused and focus on personal songs. Ixtlan doesn't need to move fast. The game isn't out yet. The world is being built.

## CONTENT WITHOUT CONTENT — THE ACTUAL ANSWER

You said: "I don't wanna grind. I just wanna make music and build worlds."

Good. Then don't grind. Here's what content without content looks like:

### The only rule: overflow, don't perform

Content that doesn't feel like content is content that comes from overflow — you made something, and sharing it is just the natural end of the process. Not a strategy. Not a calendar. The overflow.

**What overflow looks like for you:**

- You finish a song → you post it. One post. "New song." That's it.
- You write a piece of Ixtlan lore while building the game → you post it. "This is the Firebearer. He's the one who burns brightest when he's closest to losing." Image or nothing. That's it.
- You're at judo and something clicks about the connection between a throw and a song lyric → if you feel like writing it, write it. If not, don't.
- You're training kung fu and film 30 seconds because it looks good → post it or don't. Zero obligation.

No content calendar. No "3x per week." No analytics checking. The moment you start optimizing for content, it becomes a job. You're not a content creator. You're a musician and a world-builder who occasionally lets people see what you're making.

### The platforms that match this energy

**Spotify:** Where the music lives. Always. Non-negotiable. Release everything here.

**YouTube:** For Ixtlan specifically. Cinematic music with visuals — even static image videos — works on YouTube. A Ixtlan track with a piece of artwork and a lore description in the description. That's a YouTube video. No editing required.

**Anywhere else:** Only if it's zero friction. If posting somewhere feels like a task, don't do it.

### What you don't do

- No content calendar
- No "consistency strategy"
- No aesthetic grid planning
- No analytics checking for 6 months minimum
- No promoting. Sharing is not promoting. Sharing is just: this exists now.

The music finds people or it doesn't. With five years of training and a career-level voice, when the songs exist in the world, they will find people. But that can't happen while they live in FL Studio.

## THE ACTUAL ORDER OF OPERATIONS

### Right now (next 30 days)

**One thing only:** Pick one personal song from your maquetas. The one with the most feeling, not the most finished. Record the vocal cleanly. Send it to a mixing engineer. Don't think about releasing it yet — just finish it. Finishing one song breaks the paralysis more than any strategy.

Why one? Because you have a new job, El Templo to ship, judo, training, and a life. One song. That's the scope.

### After that (30-90 days)

Once one personal song is mixed and mastered, release it. No launch. Just upload to DistroKid and share it once wherever feels natural.

Then decide: is there another personal song ready? Start the same process.

Ixtlan stays paused unless a collaborator appears naturally. Don't force it. The game is the priority for Ixtlan anyway, and the game is a long game.

### Ongoing

Personal music: make → finish (with mixing engineer help) → release → forget → repeat. Whenever the creative energy is there. Not on a schedule.

Ixtlan: build the game. When a track is needed for a chapter or a character, write it. The music and the game grow together.

### The bigger picture (1-3 years)

At some point you'll have enough personal songs that an EP or album makes sense — not because you planned it, but because 8-10 songs accumulated. Release it the same way: quietly, without a campaign.

At some point Ixtlan will have a demo playable. When that happens, the music that already exists becomes the trailer soundtrack. No extra work needed.

None of this requires you to be a content creator. It requires you to finish songs.

## THE THING NOBODY TELLS YOU ABOUT HAVING TWO STYLES

You've been treating "I have two completely different styles" as a problem to solve. It isn't.

**Paul McCartney wrote "Yesterday" and "Helter Skelter."** They're not the same song. Same artist.

**Childish Gambino made "3005" and "This Is America."** Not the same style. Same person.

**Thom Yorke made Radiohead and Atoms for Peace.** Different projects. Same human.

Two styles means you contain more than one thing. That's not a branding problem. That's richness. The "personal Ricardo" music and the "Ixtlan universe" music don't need to be reconciled under one aesthetic. They need different homes — different Spotify artist pages if needed, different YouTube channels, different contexts.

People who love your personal songs might not care about Ixtlan electronic rock. People who love Ixtlan might not care about your folk songs. That's fine. You're not trying to build one audience. You're making two different things because you ARE two different things — Ricardo the person, and Ricardo the world-builder.

Stop trying to make them the same project. They're not. Let them be separate, and both will breathe easier.

Make the thing.
