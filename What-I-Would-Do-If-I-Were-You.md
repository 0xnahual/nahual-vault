## IF I WERE YOU — WHAT I WOULD DO

### El Templo — Ship It, Then Let It Live

This is the one thing that has a deadline: **this quarter.**

The app is 80% done. The last 20% has been hanging because shipping means being evaluated, and being evaluated activates the perfectionism. That's the only reason it's not out. Not technical debt. Not features. Fear.

**What I would do:**
- Set a date. 6 weeks from now. Tell one person the date.
- Spend 2-3 evenings per week finishing it. Not perfecting — finishing.
- Ship to App Store on the date. With bugs. With gaps. With the imperfections.
- After shipping: iterate based on real user feedback. 1-2 evenings per week maintaining and improving.

**What I would NOT do:**
- Build a content strategy around it
- Plan a launch campaign
- Set revenue targets for the first 6 months
- Make social media content to drive downloads

The app ships, it exists, you iterate. If it finds people, great. If not, it still exists as the IXNAH system made tangible — which has value for the game, for the books, for everything downstream. Either way, the weight of "I haven't shipped it" disappears.

**After it ships, El Templo becomes a maintenance project, not a focus project.** A few hours a week. It runs. You respond to users. You improve it. It's alive but it's not consuming you.

### Personal Music — Finish One Song Per Month

You have maquetas. You have a trained voice. You know what the songs are. The only gap is production — and for folk music, the production is minimal.

**What I would do:**

Pick one maqueta. The one that feels most alive RIGHT NOW — not the most finished, the one with the most feeling. Even if it's new, even if it's just chords and a melody that came to you yesterday.

Record the vocal and the arrangement. Keep it simple — your voice is the product. A guitar, maybe a piano, maybe one additional texture. Don't build a wall of sound. The genre is intimacy.

Send it to a mixing engineer. Fiverr, SoundBetter, whatever. $100-200 per track. Tell them: "folk, intimate, the voice is everything, minimal production." Let them finish the sound.

Master it. Upload to Spotify via DistroKid.

Share it once — wherever feels natural. One post. No campaign. No follow-up. The song exists now.

**Then do it again next month.**

Not because of a schedule — because one song per month is a pace that doesn't feel like a grind. It's just: each month, one thing gets finished. In a year, you have 10-12 songs. That's an album's worth without ever having "made an album."

**What I would NOT do:**
- Plan an album concept before the songs exist
- Try to produce everything myself in FL Studio
- Think about promotion, branding, or audience strategy
- Wait to release until I have "enough" songs
- Force songs from the depression era if they don't feel true anymore

New songs first. Write from who you are now. The old maquetas get finished IF they still feel alive. If they don't, let them rest.

### The Ixtlan Universe — Build the Game, Music Follows

Ixtlan is not a movement, not a brand, not a content strategy. It's a universe. And the game is its spine.

**What I would do:**

Accept that Ixtlan music pauses until one of two things happens:
1. A collaborator appears who wants to co-create the electronic rock sound
2. The game reaches a stage where a specific track is needed for a chapter/character

Until then, the 2 songs on Spotify exist and represent the world. That's enough for now.

**The game itself — here's how I'd approach it:**

The Nahual Game is your favorite project. It's also massive — 9 pillars, 9 archetypes, hybrid builds, a full story mode, PvP, a roguelike single-player, William's arc, the Void as final boss. This is a multi-year build.

**I would start working on it like this:**
- Saturday afternoons. 3-4 hours. No weekday pressure.
- Learn a game engine if you haven't committed to one. Godot is free, open-source, perfect for indie 2D top-down. You're an engineer — you can learn it.
- Build ONE thing first: the combat prototype. Top-down, one character, one set of attacks, one enemy. No story, no pillar system, no archetypes. Just the core loop: is the fighting fun? Does it feel frenetic? Is the gameplay addictive?
- If the core loop works, expand: add one pillar, one archetype, one chapter of story mode.
- The game grows one piece at a time. Like the music — one song at a time. One chapter at a time.

**When an Ixtlan track is needed for the game — a boss theme, a chapter intro — write it then.** The game creates the need, the music fills it. That's the natural sequence. The music doesn't lead anymore. The game does.

**What I would NOT do:**
- Try to learn animation right now
- Try to produce Ixtlan music alone without a collaborator
- Set a release date for the game
- Treat the game as a revenue strategy
- Build the game during the workweek (that's app + job time)

The game is the thing you love most. Protect it by keeping it pressure-free. Saturdays. No deadlines. Build it because William deserves to exist.

## THE THINGS TO LET GO OF

**Let go of the content strategy.** You're not a content creator. You're a musician and game developer who occasionally shows people what you're making. That's not the same thing.

**Let go of the revenue projections.** The Vision.md file has $16,000/month targets, growth phases, subscriber funnels. That's a startup roadmap, not a creative life. If the app makes money, great. If the music makes money, great. If neither does for a while, the job covers everything. The pressure of revenue targets turns every creative act into a business decision, and that's what kills the joy.

**Let go of the idea that all projects need to feed each other.** "Training creates content → content drives downloads → downloads fund the game" — this flywheel looks elegant on paper but it turns your entire life into a machine where nothing exists for its own sake. Train because you love it. Make music because it needs to exist. Build the game because William deserves to live. These don't need to be gears in a revenue machine.

**Let go of needing to know how everything connects.** Right now, today, the connections are: you're a person who trains, makes music, builds software, and is developing a game. That's it. How those connect commercially, artistically, publicly — that emerges over years. You can't see it yet. Don't force a narrative on it.

**Let go of the branding question.** "How do I promote myself, how do I present two styles, what's my artist identity" — none of this matters until music exists in the world consistently. Put out 20 songs over 2 years and the brand question answers itself because the body of work IS the brand. No strategy document can replace that.

## THE EMOTIONAL ARCHITECTURE — THE STUFF NOBODY TALKS ABOUT

### The shame around vulnerability

Your personal songs are vulnerable. You're ashamed of that. You don't say bad words anymore. "De chiquito" feels like old skin.

Here's what I'd remember if I were you: vulnerability is not weakness. You know this intellectually. But emotionally, the crypto collapse taught you that being seen = being attacked. So anything that shows the soft parts triggers the reflex.

The reflex is quieter now — you said the phobic responses are gone. But the residual discomfort around being HEARD singing something honest? That's still there. And it's the last layer.

You don't push through this. You just keep putting songs out, one at a time, and each time nothing bad happens, the layer gets thinner.

### The isolation question

You're building a life with more humans in it — judo handles the community part. The partner will come from a life that includes people, not from a strategy.

The one thing I'd add: **find one person you can be genuinely honest with.** Not about your system. About your life. "I'm building a game and I don't know if it'll ever work." "I have all these songs and I'm scared to release them." "I love fighting and I don't know why." That level of honesty, with one human, is worth more than 200 files of self-dialogue.

### The "I don't know what I'm doing" feeling

You've built this entire vault to feel like you know what you're doing. Frameworks, roadmaps, archetypes, phases. And then you sit in front of me and say "I have no idea how to do this."

Good. That's honest. Nobody knows how to do this. There is no playbook for "30-year-old Mexican AI engineer who lost everything in crypto, transformed through DMT and kung fu, sings folk music and builds mythological video games." That person doesn't have a template. You're making it up as you go.

The frameworks were useful for getting you here. They gave structure to chaos during the hardest years. But you don't need them anymore — not at the same density. You need to do things and see what happens. Write a song and release it and see how it feels. Ship the app and see what users do. Build a combat prototype and see if it's fun. Go to judo and see who you meet.

The clarity you're looking for doesn't come from more planning. It comes from doing things and watching what emerges.
